"The Birth of a New Age (Again)"

MLK Day 2026

Today we honor Dr. King. And I've been sitting with a speech of his that doesn't get quoted enough—not "I Have a Dream," but "The Birth of a New Age," delivered in 1956.

"We stand today between two worlds—the dying old and the emerging new."

That was 70 years ago. And yet—here we are again.

Standing between worlds. Feeling the tension. Wondering if we're progressing or regressing.

King had an answer for that too:

"Far from representing retrogression or tragic hopelessness, the present tension represents the necessary pains that accompany the birth of anything new."

Birth pains. Not death throes.

I've been thinking about this through the lens of physics. At the Big Bang, everything was together—then collision. And from that collision came stars, planets, oceans, life. The universe doesn't create sameness. It creates diversity. It experiments. It collides. And something new emerges.

King understood this. He said the goal was never retaliation. Never domination. The goal was "the creation of a beloved community… a society where people will live together as brothers and sisters."

He called it reconciliation. Redemption.

I've been calling it resonance.

The sociologist Hartmut Rosa describes resonance as a relationship where "subject and world are mutually affected and transformed." Not one side winning. Not compromise. Mutual transformation.

That's what King was after. Not victory over an opponent—but a collision that creates something better than either side alone.

Every meeting point between people who are different is a chance for that. Immigration is collision. Policy is collision. Cultures, languages, histories, hopes—meeting, clashing, reshaping one another.

Each of those moments can harden us. Or humanize us.

David Brooks recently wrote about "The Great Detachment"—how we chose autonomy over connection and ended up with achievement and anxiety instead of belonging. King saw this coming too. In that same 1956 speech he warned that we must rise above the "narrow confines of our individualistic concerns" and develop a broader concern for all humanity.

The old order was dying then. It's dying now.

The question is what we birth in its place.

King believed—deeply—that "justice eventually rules in this world… the forces of darkness cannot permanently conquer the forces of light."

I don't know if I have his faith. But I have this:

The universe is still creating. Still diversifying. Still running experiments to find resonance.

Our job was never to make one part of humanity into another part of humanity. Our job is to figure out how to resonate.

That was King's work. It's still ours.

The old order is dying. The new order is being born.

Let's make sure we're midwives, not mourners.

Happy MLK Day.
